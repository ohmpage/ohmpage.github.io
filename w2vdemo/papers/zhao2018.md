# Gender Bias in Coreference Resolution: Evaluation and Debiasing Methods

## Citation

Zhao, J., Wang, T., Yatskar, M., Ordonez, V., & Chang, K.-W. (2018). Gender bias in coreference resolution: Evaluation and debiasing methods. In *Proceedings of the 2018 Conference of the North American Chapter of the Association for Computational Linguistics: Human Language Technologies (NAACL-HLT)*, 15–20.

- **arXiv:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.06876
- **PDF:** https://arxiv.org/pdf/1804.06876

## Key Finding

Word embedding bias is not just a curiosity — it propagates into actual software behavior. The authors study coreference resolution systems (software that determines who "she" or "he" refers to in a sentence) and show that all major systems — rule-based, feature-rich, and neural — are significantly more accurate when resolving pronouns to *stereotypically expected* occupations than to unexpected ones. The average gap is 21 F1 points, a very large effect. They introduce the WinoBias benchmark to standardize measurement of this effect.

## Memorable Examples

Coreference systems perform much better on stereotypical pairings like:
- "The **doctor** asked the nurse to hand her the clipboard." → system correctly identifies *she* refers to the doctor
- "The **nurse** asked the doctor to hurry up because she was ready." → system correctly identifies *she* refers to the nurse

But perform poorly on anti-stereotypical pairings:
- "The **nurse** asked the doctor to hand her the clipboard." → system *incorrectly* infers *her* refers to the doctor (the "male" occupation)

## Policy Significance

Coreference resolution is used in contract analysis software, legal document review, HR systems that parse résumés, and predictive text tools. When a legal AI tool parses "the engineer ... she" and defaults to assigning *she* to a lower-status actor in the sentence, it is replicating occupational gender segregation in the interpretation of legal documents. This has concrete implications for any AI used in document review or contract drafting.

## Why This Paper Matters

This paper closes the loop between "bias is encoded in embeddings" (Bolukbasi, Caliskan) and "bias causes real errors in deployed software." The WinoBias benchmark it introduces is now a standard evaluation tool. The 21-point accuracy gap is a concrete, audience-accessible number: "the software is 21% less accurate when a woman is the doctor."
