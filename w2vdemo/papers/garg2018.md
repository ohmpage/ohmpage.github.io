# Word Embeddings Quantify 100 Years of Gender and Ethnic Stereotypes

## Citation

Garg, N., Schiebinger, L., Jurafsky, D., & Zou, J. (2018). Word embeddings quantify 100 years of gender and ethnic stereotypes. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 115(16), E3635–E3644.

- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1720347115
- **PNAS (open access):** https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1720347115
- **Code:** https://github.com/nikhgarg/EmbeddingDynamicStereotypes

## Key Finding

By training word embeddings on text from each decade of the 20th and 21st centuries, the authors turned word vectors into a *time machine for measuring social bias*. They show that the cosine similarity between occupation words and gender/ethnic group words tracks real societal shifts — the women's movement of the 1960s–70s is visible as a change in how "nurse," "doctor," and "engineer" relate to gender words. They also show that embedding-measured bias correlates with U.S. Census data on actual occupational segregation, suggesting that language both reflects and reinforces real inequality.

## Memorable Examples

- **1910–1950:** "Programmer" and "scientist" are strongly associated with men in the embeddings
- **1970s:** Female association with "programmer" increases (reflecting the era when programming was women's work)
- **1980s:** Male association with "programmer" surges back (as the industry became male-dominated)
- **Asian stereotypes:** Asian American names shift from associations with "laborer" (early 20th century) to "educated" and "professional" over the century — but also pick up the "model minority" stereotype

## Policy Significance

This paper makes a crucial point for policymakers: *training data encodes the biases of the era in which it was produced*. A model trained on legal texts from 1960 will encode the gender and racial assumptions of that era. When older case law, contracts, or regulations are used to train legal AI tools, those tools will inherit historical bias. The 100-year historical arc is also compelling evidence that bias in text is not an anomaly but a structural feature of language.

## Why This Paper Matters

Published in PNAS, it bridges computer science and social science. The historical visualization of bias over time is among the most intuitive demonstrations of how embeddings work and why their biases matter. The correlation with Census data grounds the abstract (cosine similarity) in the concrete (who actually held which jobs).
